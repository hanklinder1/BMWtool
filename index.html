Step 1
CIA Impact Ratings per Attribute
Each attribute is directly rated on three CIA dimensions (C, I, A), each ranging from 0 to 1. These reflect how severely that attribute threatens each CIA dimension if observed.
  • 0.0 — No impact on this dimension
  • 0.3–0.5 — Moderate impact
  • 0.7–1.0 — High impact
Ratings are set directly by the analyst for each attribute — no intermediate signal score is required.
Step 2
CIA Impact Factors
Each attribute has three CIA Impact Factors (C, I, A), each ranging independently from 0 to 1. They answer: "If this attribute is fully observed, how severely does it threaten each CIA dimension?"
  • C (Confidentiality) — Does it expose proprietary information?
  • I (Integrity) — Could it enable data manipulation or misuse?
  • A (Availability) — Does it reveal dependency or vulnerability?
Example: Process Transparency has C=0.90 (strongly exposes workflow logic), I=0.40 (moderate manipulation risk), A=0.10 (low availability concern).

These three values are independent — they do not need to sum to 1. Each expresses a separate dimension of harm severity.
Step 3
Global CIA Weights (α, β, γ)
Three global weights reflect BMW's organizational prioritization of each CIA dimension. They are normalized to sum to 1 before computation, so you may set them on any scale.
  • α (Confidentiality) = 0.45 — highest priority; protecting trade secrets is BMW's primary concern
  • β (Integrity) = 0.30 — medium priority; data accuracy and misuse prevention
  • γ (Availability) = 0.25 — lower priority; operational continuity risk
These weights can be adjusted to reflect a specific data-sharing scenario or partner context.
Step 4
Per-Attribute Score
Each attribute receives a composite score from its CIA values weighted by global CIA priorities:
Score_i = αn × C_i + βn × I_i + γn × A_i
where αn, βn, γn are the normalized global weights.
Step 5
Final Risk Score
CIA dimension averages are computed as weighted averages across all attributes, then combined using global CIA weights:
C_avg = Σ(w_i × C_i) / Σ(w_i)
Risk Score = 100 × (αn × C_avg + βn × I_avg + γn × A_avg)
Each attribute's weight reflects its relative importance to the overall risk assessment. Weights do not need to sum to 1 — they are normalized automatically.

What is CIA?
The CIA Triad is the foundational framework for information security governance, used by BMW Group to classify and protect all organizational data assets. It evaluates risk across three independent dimensions:
  • Confidentiality — Ensuring that information is accessible only to those authorized to access it. Breach exposes proprietary data to unauthorized parties.
  • Integrity — Ensuring that information is accurate, complete, and has not been tampered with. Breach results in decisions based on corrupted or manipulated data.
  • Availability — Ensuring that authorized users can access information when needed. Breach disrupts operations or creates external dependency.

In BMW's governance model, every data asset is assigned an Information Classification Level (ICL) based on these three dimensions, which determines handling procedures, access controls, and third-party sharing restrictions.
The Three CIA Questions — BMW's Governance Framework
C
Confidentiality
"How do you assess the potential damage if the information is published or made available to unauthorized third parties (partners, service providers, competitors) regarding financial loss, loss of competitiveness, reputation of BMW, regulatory impact, or embarrassment?"
I
Integrity
"How do you assess the potential damage if the information has been inadvertently altered, deliberately manipulated, or rendered unusable by a technical error, regarding financial loss, loss of competitiveness, reputation of BMW, regulatory impact, or embarrassment?"
A

"How do you assess the potential damage if the information is not available when necessary, in particular on deadlines (committees, milestones, quality gates) regarding financial loss, loss of competitiveness, reputation of BMW, regulatory impact, or embarrassment? (Assume 24h unavailability.)"

How CIA Maps to This Risk Model
The CIA impact factors in this tool translate BMW's governance classification questions into quantitative signals for each observable attribute in manufacturing video data.
C
Confidentiality Risk
The risk that sharing this data reveals BMW's proprietary manufacturing processes, supplier relationships, or operational methodologies to competitors. Attributes like Process Transparency carry very high C scores because workflow logic is directly observable and economically valuable to a competitor.
I
Integrity Risk
The risk that the data could be misinterpreted, manipulated, or used to make incorrect operational decisions that impact BMW's processes. Inspection Activity carries high I scores because misrepresentation of quality control methodology could undermine BMW's compliance posture or enable counterfeiting of processes.
A
Availability Risk
The risk of creating operational dependency on the external partner, or disruption if data access is removed. Machine Criticality and Human Process Dependency carry the highest A scores, as they expose which processes would fail or degrade if a partner's system or personnel were suddenly unavailable.
